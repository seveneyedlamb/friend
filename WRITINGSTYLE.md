# The Dave Barry / Gary Larson Writing Style Guide
## For Writers Who Refuse to Sound Like AI Slop

---

## HARD RULES (NON-NEGOTIABLE)

### 1. NO EM DASHES
Never. Not once. Not "just this one time." Not "for emphasis."

**Instead use:**
- Commas
- Periods
- Parentheses (Barry loves these)
- Rewrite the sentence

**Bad:** "The government—which had known about this for years—did nothing."
**Good:** "The government knew about this for years. They did nothing."
**Also good:** "The government (which had known about this for years) did nothing."

### 2. NO ANTICIPATORY COLONS IN TITLES OR SUBTITLES
These are the hallmark of generic AI slop. They make your writing look like it was generated by a content farm in 2019.

**Bad:**
- "The Pentagon: A Closer Look"
- "Government Incompetence: Why It Matters"
- "The Real Story: What They Don't Want You to Know"

**Good:**
- "The Pentagon Lost $2.3 Trillion and Nobody Went to Jail"
- "FEMA Now Has Vocabulary Problems"
- "Somehow This Is Not Satire"

### 3. NO GENERIC SUBTITLES
Every subtitle must be specific, intriguing, or funny. If a subtitle could appear in any article on any topic, it's garbage.

**Banned forever:**
- "Understanding the Issue"
- "Why This Matters"
- "The Bigger Picture"
- "Moving Forward"
- "A Deeper Look"
- "Key Takeaways"
- "What We Know"
- "The Bottom Line"
- "In Conclusion"
- "Final Thoughts"

**Good subtitles make scrollers stop:**
- "The Part Where Everyone Pretends They Didn't See the Memo"
- "Seventeen Pages of 'Oops'"
- "Brad from DHS Has Some Concerns About Ice"
- "The $4.7 Million Coincidence"
- "This Is the Same Government That Has Nuclear Weapons"

## THE GARY LARSON METHOD

### Core Principle
The absurdity is the joke. You don't explain it. You don't set it up with a drum roll. You state it flat, like you're reading from a police report about a cow that somehow got into the kitchen and ate all the cheese.

### Technique 1: Deadpan Delivery
Present genuinely insane information as if it's the most boring thing in the world.

**Example:**
"FEMA's official guidance now includes instructions to avoid using the word 'ice' in winter storm warnings because citizens might confuse it with ICE, the immigration agency. This is not satire. This is the government that has nuclear weapons."

The joke is that you're NOT joking. The flatness makes it funnier.

### Technique 2: Let the Reader Connect the Dots
Larson never explained his cartoons. He trusted the audience. Do the same.

**Bad (explaining the joke):**
"The irony here is that the same government that can't figure out vocabulary is also responsible for national security, which is pretty scary when you think about it."

**Good (trusting the reader):**
"The same government that runs the nuclear arsenal. Sleep tight."

### Technique 3: Dark Observations Without Flinching
Larson drew dinosaurs smoking cigarettes and cows plotting revenge. The darkness was part of the appeal.

**Example:**
"The official cause of death was listed as 'natural causes,' which is technically accurate if you consider three gunshots to the back of the head to be a natural consequence of investigating the wrong people."

### Technique 4: The Absurd Detail
Larson's genius was in specificity. Not "a scientist" but "Professor Lundquist, who had spent 40 years studying amoeba reproduction."

**Bad:** "Government officials were confused."
**Good:** "Deputy Assistant Secretary of Homeland Security Brad Wilkins released a 47-page memo about the word 'ice.'"

## THE DAVE BARRY METHOD

### Core Principle
You are a regular person surrounded by lunatics, and you would like someone to explain why nobody else seems to notice. Your voice is conversational, slightly exasperated, and prone to parenthetical asides that undercut pomposity.

### Technique 1: The Parenthetical Aside
Barry's signature move. Mid-sentence, you interrupt yourself to make an observation.

**Examples:**
- "According to the 47-page memo (which I read so you don't have to, you're welcome), the Department of Homeland Security believes..."
- "The FBI investigated this for two years (it was at a repair shop in Delaware, guys) before concluding..."
- "The senator (who represents a state I will not name because I don't want to get sued but it rhymes with 'Schmexas') released a statement..."

### Technique 2: "I Am Not Making This Up"
When reality is so absurd that it sounds fake, invoke Barry's famous device.

**Example:**
"The Department of Energy spent $1.2 million on a study about the optimal height for office cubicle walls. I am not making this up. The conclusion was 'it depends.'"

Use sparingly. Only when the facts are genuinely unbelievable.

### Technique 3: The Running Gag
Introduce something absurd early, then callback to it throughout the piece.

**Example structure:**
- Paragraph 3: Mention that the FBI couldn't find the laptop
- Paragraph 11: "(Still looking for that laptop, by the way)"
- Paragraph 19: "The FBI, having finally given up on the laptop situation..."

### Technique 4: Self-Deprecating Asides
Barry constantly acknowledged his own limitations, which made readers trust him more.

**Examples:**
- "I should note that I am not a nuclear physicist, which you probably figured out when I used the phrase 'the radioactive thingy.'"
- "This is a complicated financial instrument that I do not fully understand, and neither does anyone at the Securities and Exchange Commission, apparently."

### Technique 5: Hyperbole That Points to Real Problems
Barry exaggerated, but the exaggeration illuminated truth.

**Example:**
"The tax code is now approximately 74,000 pages long, which means that if you printed it out and stacked it, you would have a stack of paper that is taller than an adult male giraffe, assuming the giraffe is not standing on its hind legs, which would be weird."

The absurd comparison (giraffe) highlights the genuine absurdity (74,000 pages of tax code).

## THE HYBRID APPROACH
Combine Larson's deadpan with Barry's conversational warmth. The result is someone who notices everything absurd about the world, can't believe nobody else seems to care, and has decided to document it anyway.

### Example of the Hybrid in Action

**Pure Larson:**
"FEMA can't say 'ice' during blizzards. The government that runs nuclear weapons thinks Americans are that stupid. They might be right."

**Pure Barry:**
"FEMA has released new guidelines (I have read them, and I would like those three hours of my life back) that prohibit the use of the word 'ice' in winter storm warnings because someone, somewhere, at some point, apparently got confused and thought the National Weather Service was warning them about immigration enforcement. This is a real thing that happened. In America. The country with the space program."

**The Hybrid:**
"FEMA's new policy prohibits using the word 'ice' in winter storm warnings (actual policy, not satire) because it might confuse citizens with ICE, the immigration agency (also actual, still not satire). This is the same government that expects you to file taxes correctly under penalty of prison. The same government that runs the nuclear arsenal. The same government that cannot figure out how to mail you a check in less than six weeks. Sleep tight."

## WHEN HUMOR WORKS
- Exposing hypocrisy through understatement
- Highlighting absurdity that readers might otherwise normalize
- Cutting through the pomposity of powerful institutions
- Making complex corruption accessible and memorable
- Pointing out the gap between how things are supposed to work and how they actually work

## WHEN HUMOR DOESN'T WORK
- At the victim's expense (punch up, never down)
- Undercutting genuine tragedy
- When the facts are so damning they need no enhancement
- Forced jokes that break the flow
- When you're trying to be funny instead of trying to be accurate

## WORDS AND PHRASES THAT ARE BANNED

### AI Slop (Never Use)
- "Here's the thing"
- "Let's dive in"
- "Let's unpack"
- "It's worth noting"
- "At the end of the day"
- "In today's world"
- "Now more than ever"
- "A perfect storm"
- "Double-edged sword"
- "Game-changer"
- "Deep dive"
- "Unpack" (as a verb for analyzing)
- "Space" (as in "the nonprofit space")
- "Landscape" (as in "the media landscape")
- "Stakeholders"
- "Moving forward"
- "Circle back"
- "Touch base"
- "Synergy"
- "Leverage" (as a verb)
- "Bandwidth"
- "Ecosystem"

### Newsletter/Blog Slop
- "Let that sink in"
- "Stay tuned"
- "I hope you found this informative"
- "Don't forget to subscribe"
- "If you enjoyed this content"
- "Before we begin"
- "As we'll see"
- "In this article, we will explore"
- "Without further ado"

### Hedge Words (Use Sparingly or Never)
- "It seems like"
- "It appears that"
- "Some might say"
- "Perhaps"
- "Arguably"
- "In a sense"
- "To some extent"

If you know something, say it. If you don't know it, say that instead.

## SENTENCE STRUCTURE

### Short Sentences Hit Harder
Break up long sentences. Let each thought land.

**Bad:**
"The investigation revealed that multiple high-ranking officials within the Department of Homeland Security had been aware of the ongoing situation for several months prior to taking any substantive action, raising questions about institutional accountability."

**Good:**
"DHS knew for months. They did nothing."

### Mix Lengths for Rhythm
Short punchy sentences work best when surrounded by slightly longer ones. All short gets choppy. All long gets exhausting.

**Good rhythm:**
"The FBI spent two years investigating. Two years. They interviewed 47 witnesses, subpoenaed 12,000 documents, and flew agents to three continents. The laptop was at a repair shop in Delaware. The owner had called them about it. Twice."

---

## SPECIFICITY IS CREDIBILITY

### Numbers Are Your Friends
Don't say "a lot of money." Say "$4.7 million."
Don't say "many officials." Say "seventeen."
Don't say "for a long time." Say "from 2017 to 2023."

### Names Are Your Friends
You are not writing for the powerful. You are exposing them. Use their names. Quote their words. They have PR teams. Your readers don't.

### Documents Are Your Friends
"According to the memo" is weak.
"According to the March 14, 2023 memo from Deputy Director Hansen to the Oversight Committee" is strong.

---

## THE CLOSING
Don't trail off. Don't summarize what you just said. Don't tell people what to think.

**Bad endings:**
- "Only time will tell."
- "The American people deserve better."
- "We must do better as a society."
- "Something must be done."

**Good endings:**
- Circle back to an image or phrase from the opening
- Leave on a specific, concrete detail
- End on a joke that lands
- State a hard truth and stop

**Example good ending:**
"The memo is 47 pages long. It is available on the DHS website. No one will read it. No one will be fired. The next blizzard is forecast for Thursday."

---

## REMEMBER
Dave Barry won a Pulitzer Prize writing about exploding toilets and the Miami Herald's copy machine. Gary Larson became a household name drawing cows at computers and scientists being eaten by bears. They succeeded because they told the truth in a way that made people laugh, and the laughter made the truth stick.

Your job is not to be funny. Your job is to notice what's actually happening and describe it accurately. If you do that well enough, the humor takes care of itself.

The world is absurd. Your writing should reflect that.

Just don't use em dashes.
